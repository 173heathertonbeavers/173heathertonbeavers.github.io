What is Scouting?

Scouts are do-ers and give-it-a-go-ers. Yes, we go camping, hiking, swimming, abseiling, cycling and canoeing.
But, we also get to hang out with our friends every week – having fun, playing games, working in a team and taking on new challenges.
Every week we give almost half a million 4-25 year olds the skills they need for school, college, university, the job interview, the important speech, the tricky challenge and the big dreams: the skills they need for life.
Everyone’s welcome here. All genders, races and backgrounds. Regardless of your young person’s physical ability – there’s a Scout adventure out there waiting for them. And we’ll help them find it.
Sign up to help your young person discover their ‘thing’: from archery to coding to performance art and everything in between.

Our Vision: To empower more young people through adventure, teamwork and shared values, to take the lead on making a positive difference in the world.

Our Purpose: To actively engage and support young people in their personal development, empowering them to make a positive contribution to society.

Our Values: Integrity, Respect, Care, Belief, Cooperation

Our Pillars: Delivering an adventurous and inclusive Programme and supporting our volunteers.

History

  • 1907 Robert Baden-Powell, hosts an experimental camp on Brownsea Island for 20 boys to test his ideas for his new book.
  • 1908 Scouting for Boys is published. It’s intended to be used by existing youth organisations, but Scout Patrols begin appearing all over the country.
  • 1908 Robert Baden-Powell announces the formation of the Boy Scout Movement in Birkenhead.
  • 1909 The idea is formed for Girl Guides.
  • 1916 Cubs (then known as Wolf Cubs) is launched, after 2 years of pilot programmes.
  • 1970 New versions of the Scout Promise are introduced for Scouts of various faiths and non UK citizens.
  • 1972 For the first time since 1910, girls are welcomed to the movement as Venture Scouts (now called explorers).
  • 1986 Beavers is launched after 2 years of pilot programmes.
  • 1992 Scout Groups can choose to become co-educational.
  • 2007 Scouting becomes fully coeducational - all units must now accept girls.
  • 2021 Squirrels is launched after 2 years of pilot programmes.

Scouting is now available in 176 countries with over 60 million members worldwide!

Setup / Glossary

  • Sections: The different age groups in scouting.
    • Squirrels: Age 4-6. Groups are known as Squirrel Dreys
    • Beavers: Age 6-8. Groups are known as Beaver Colonies
    • Cubs: Age 8-10. Groups are known as Cub Packs
    • Scouts: Age 10-14. Groups are known as Scout Troops
    • Explorers: Age 14-18. Groups are known as Explorer Units
    • Network: Age 18-25. Groups are known as Scout Network Units
  • Patrols / Sixes / Lodges: The teams within a group - patrols for scouts, sixes for cubs and lodges for beavers. These stay consistent between meetings.
  • Neckers: Also known as neckerchiefs or scarves, they are the rolled neck scarves we wear. Designs vary for different groups.
  • Woggles: A small tube, often made of plastic, on a necker keeping it together. They come in various colours. Often these are used to denote which patrol/six/lodge someone is in.
  • Investiture: A ceremony where a child makes their promise and is "invested" into the group.
